About

Bridging ocean science and machine learning

I'm a PhD student in the Department of Earth and Environmental Engineering at Columbia University. My research focuses on understanding and improving how we model the exchanges of heat and momentum between the ocean and atmosphere — processes that are critical for weather forecasting and climate projection.

Before starting my PhD, I worked for two years as an engineering consultant in process, energy, and environmental engineering. I hold a B.S. in Chemical Engineering from Washington University in St. Louis.

My current work is supported by the National Defense Science and Engineering Graduate (NDSEG) Fellowship. I combine observational data, remote sensing, and physics-informed machine learning to build better parameterizations of air-sea fluxes.

Research

Current focus areas

Exploring the physical exchanges at the ocean-atmosphere boundary and their implications for climate and renewable energy.

Air-Sea Flux Parameterization

Developing improved parameterizations of heat, moisture, and momentum fluxes at the ocean-atmosphere interface. By combining in situ observations, satellite remote sensing, and physics-informed machine learning, I work to reduce biases in bulk flux algorithms used by weather and climate models.

Heat Flux Momentum Transfer Bulk Algorithms ML Parameterization

Offshore Wind Energy

Investigating how air-sea interactions influence the marine atmospheric boundary layer in regions planned for offshore wind development. Understanding these dynamics is essential for accurate wind resource assessment, turbine performance prediction, and the long-term viability of offshore wind farms.

Boundary Layer Wind Resource Renewable Energy Coupled Modeling
